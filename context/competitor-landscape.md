# Competitor Landscape

---

## Instructions

1. Document your main competitors and alternatives
2. Be honest about strengths and weaknesses
3. Focus on differentiation, not bashing competitors
4. Update as the competitive landscape evolves

## Handling Competitive Objections

### "We're already using [Competitor A]"

```
Response: "Makes sense — [Competitor A] is solid. Out of curiosity, 
how's [specific feature/outcome] working for you? 

The reason I ask is that most teams who switch to us do so because 
[specific differentiator]. Worth a quick comparison?"
```

### "We looked at you before and went with [Competitor B]"

```
Response: "Totally understand. A lot has changed since then — 
[specific improvement]. Would it be worth a fresh look, or is 
timing still not right?"
```

### "What makes you different from [Competitor]?"

```
Response: "Great question. The biggest difference is [key differentiator]. 
In practice, this means [tangible benefit]. 

For example, [Customer] switched from [Competitor] and saw [result]."
```

### "You're more expensive than [Competitor]"

```
Response: "You're right that our list price is higher. What we've 
found is that [value justification — e.g., 'teams see X result that 
more than covers the difference'].

[Customer] calculated their ROI at [X] after switching from [cheaper option]."
```

## Messaging Do's and Don'ts

### Do

- Acknowledge competitor strengths ("They're solid at X")
- Focus on your unique value
- Use customer evidence
- Be confident, not defensive

### Don't

- Bash competitors directly
- Make unverifiable claims
- Lie about competitor weaknesses
- Spend too much time on competition
